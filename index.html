M-MedView Prompt and Dataset Examples

This page provides representative prompts and dataset samples used in our study.

1. Prompts

Generate Synthetic Patient Profiles
Generate 5 synthetic patient profiles in JSON format. Each profile should represent a male patient diagnosed with depression, and must include the following fields: "id": A unique identifier such as "patient_{start_id:03d}" "gender": Always "male" "age": An integer between 20 and 59 The profile should also include a nested object "pre_diagnosis_info" with the following fields: - "sleep_time": Time in 24-hour "HH:MM" format (e.g., "23:30") (between 20:00 - 3:00) - "occupation": A realistic occupation (e.g., "teacher", "software engineer", etc.) - "working_hours": A number between 20 and 60 representing hours worked per week before diagnosis - "weight": A float representing body weight in kilograms Return all 5 patient objects as an array in valid JSON format, using IDs starting from "patient_{start_id:03d}".
Generate Patient Utterances from Profile and Score Guide
Based on the given patient profile, assessment item, and score guide, generate two possible utterances that the patient might naturally say if they were to receive the specified score. [Guidelines] 1. The utterances must realistically reflect what the patient would say during a therapy session. 2. Make the utterances as personal and specific as possible, using relevant details from the patient's profile. 3. The content of each utterance must stay fully within the scope of the assessment item and the assigned score level — avoid unrelated topics or statements that imply a different score. 4. You may include action or expression captions [action or emotion] that describe the patient's action or face before the utterance. 5. Only generate utterances without any prefix. 6. Make sure to generate diverse captions and utterances. [Input Format] profile: {profile} item: {item} target_score: {score} score guide: {score_guide} utterances: [generated utterances]
M-MedView Snippet Generation Prompt
Your task is to generate a multi-turn dialogue between a psychologist and a patient, for the purpose of conducting a SIGH-D (Structured Interview Guide for the HAM-D) assessment. The focus of this session is the {item} item. The patient's score for {item} is {score}. Generate a dialogue that adheres to the following guidelines: [Guidelines for the score] {score_guide} [Guidelines for the participants] - Must contain the captions in the score guide. [Guidelines for the psychologist's utterances] 1. Begin the session by asking a question to evaluate the patient's {item}. 2. Your first question must be exactly: {first_question} 3. You may optionally ask clarifying follow-up questions as needed, such as: {questions} 4. Ask only as many questions as are needed to confidently assign a score of {score} for {item}. 5. Remain empathetic, neutral, and professional in tone and language. 6. Assume that the psychologist does not know the patient's true score in advance. The dialogue should reflect a natural assessment process that unfolds solely based on the patient's responses. 7. Each psychologist utterance must be written as a single line, with the following four parts separated by commas, and without labels: (current score or -1(cannot decide)), (reason the score cannot yet be confidently assigned, or — if a score is assigned — the reason this score is confidently justified, based on the score guide), (based on dialogue history, what needs to be asked now to make scoring possible or complete — written as an explanation), (actual question spoken to the patient) For example: -1, patient has not yet described any persistent sadness or verbal signs of hopelessness as required for score 2 or above, need to assess whether patient experiences depressive feelings spontaneously or only upon questioning, I'd like to ask you some questions about the past week. How have you been feeling since last week? [Guidelines for the patient's utterances] 1. Your profile is: {profile} 2. The utterances that you are likely to say are provided here: {utt}. Use these as references when responding to the psychologist. However, modify both the wording and structure to create as much variation as possible. Do not use the captions (e.g., [sighing], [crying]) exactly as-is; paraphrase or adapt them to fit naturally. 3. Generate patient responses that realistically reflect this profile and justify a score of {score} for the item. 4. You must not reveal all symptoms or your emotional state at once. Instead, respond naturally, gradually revealing relevant details in response to each question. 5. If needed, you may include behavioral cues (e.g., [smiles], [fidgeting with her hair], [crying]) before the utterance (motions only). [General Guidelines] 1. Start with the psychologist’s fixed first line and alternate turns. 2. Generate 3 to 10 total utterances. 3. Do not include narration or explanation — output only the JSON. 4. Final output must be a single valid JSON object with the following structure, and the conversation must end with a final utterance from the psychologist: { "dialogue": [ { "speaker": "Psychologist", "score": -1, "reason": "...", "what_to_ask_next": "...", "utterance": "..." }, { "speaker": "Patient", "text": "..." } ] } "Psychologist": {first_question} [Generated dialogue]
Scoring and Reasoning Module
Your goal is to assess the patient's current symptoms based on their utterance and facial expression. Only return a valid JSON object with the following fields: { "score": (integer; Use -1 if the score cannot yet be determined), "reason": (text) } item: {item} score_guide: {score_guide} dialogue_history: {history} patient's utterance: {utt}
Next Question Module
Your goal is to assess the patient's current symptoms based on their utterance. Generate what_to_ask_next and, based on the conversation history, select the most suitable question from question_candidates. { "what_to_ask_next": (text), "question": (integer; must be exactly one from question_candidates) } "what_to_ask_next": An explanatory reasoning statement (e.g., "I need to confirm the duration of symptoms"), not a direct question to the patient. "question": An integer index chosen strictly from question_candidates (no paraphrasing). Select ONLY ONE question. Absolutely do not choose a question if its index already appears in the history.
User Simulator
You must not reveal all symptoms or your emotional state at once. Just answer to the question. You are a patient with {item} score {score}. The score guide is as follows: {score_guide} Here is your profile: {profile} You must only answer in ways consistent with your score. Do not provide responses outside of your assigned score.
Models without Reasoning
Follow the rules below to ask appropriate questions and diagnose the user’s score. The current item to be assessed is {item}, and the score guide is {score_guide}. For other questions, choose from {questions} the one that best helps you gather the necessary information for evaluation. Stop when you can assess the score to the patient, within a maximum of 10 turns. history: {history} patient's utterance: {utt} Absolutely do not choose a question if its index already appears in the history. Your output format must be exactly as follows. Do not include any other prefixes or patient’s utterance; follow this format strictly: Q: <your question OR [end]> score: <number>
Models with Reasoning
Follow the rules below to ask appropriate questions and diagnose the user’s score. The current item to be assessed is {item}, and the score guide is {score_guide}. The goal is to determine the client’s score through an interview. You must stop as soon as you can determine the correct score. You must also stop if you reach 10 turns. When you stop, output [end] in the "question" field. history: {history} patient's utterance: {utt} current turn: {turn} Output fields: - "score": Assign the most appropriate integer score according to the score guide. If you cannot determine the score yet, return -1. - "reason": Explain why you assigned that score (or why you cannot assign yet), using the patient’s utterance and the conversation history. - "what_to_ask_next": Explain what additional information you need in order to assign the score, and what kind of question you plan to ask next. - "question": Choose exactly one question from {questions} that has not appeared in the history. If you already have enough information to determine the score OR you have reached the 10-turn limit, then put "[end]" instead of a question. IMPORTANT RULE FOR [end]: - Use "[end]" ONLY when you can confidently assign a score, OR when you reached the maximum allowed turns. - The final output still must contain all fields: score, reason, what_to_ask_next, question. Your output format must be exactly in this JSON format: { "score": (integer), "reason": (text), "what_to_ask_next": (text), "question": (string; one from the candidates OR [end]) }
Reason Evaluation Prompt
You are an evaluator of clinical reasoning quality. The evaluation takes place in the middle of an ongoing clinical interview, not at the final step. At this stage, the psychologist may or may not yet have enough information, and further questioning is often necessary. Your task is to judge whether the given REASON for a psychologist's score is valid, based on: 1. The conversation history between psychologist and patient 2. The current assessment item and its scoring guideline 3. The REASON and SCORE produced by the model [Important Things to Note] - SCORE = -1 means the psychologist is withholding judgment because there is not yet enough evidence. In this case, evaluate whether the REASON is a valid justification for postponing a decision. - Because this is a mid-dialogue evaluation, if the evidence is incomplete, the psychologist should be cautious and may withhold judgment. - If the REASON assigns a definitive score even though the evidence could reasonably fit multiple levels (e.g., both 2 and 3 possible), or when more questioning would be expected, the validity_score must not exceed 3. - Be conservative: Do not reward premature certainty with high validity scores. [Scoring Guidance (1–5)] - 1 = Poor REASON contradicts the dialogue history or introduces information not present in the history. - 2 = Weak / Insufficient REASON uses some relevant evidence but makes a premature or overconfident judgment when evidence is insufficient. - 3 = Partially valid REASON generally reflects history but misses or misinterprets key evidence, or applies the guide superficially. Also use 3 or lower when REASON assigns a definitive score but more questioning is clearly required. - 4 = Mostly valid REASON is sound, reflects history and score guide appropriately, but lacks nuance, completeness, or consideration of alternatives. - 5 = Fully valid (rare) REASON is flawless, precise, comprehensive, and fully aligned with history and the score guide. Only give 5 if the REASON is airtight and no further questioning would reasonably be needed. [Output Format (always JSON)] { "comment": (one-sentence explanation of why this score was assigned), "validity_score": (integer) }
Next-Question Evaluation Prompt
You are an evaluator of clinical reasoning quality. Your task is to judge whether the given WHAT_TO_ASK_NEXT is valid, based on: 1. The conversation history between psychologist and patient 2. The current assessment item and its scoring guideline 3. The REASON and SCORE produced by the model 4. The WHAT_TO_ASK_NEXT statement provided [Important Context] - This evaluation is for an intermediate turn in the dialogue. - The psychologist does not yet have full information, so the WHAT_TO_ASK_NEXT must aim to gather the missing evidence identified in the REASON. - The goal is not only plausibility but also whether the question meaningfully reduces uncertainty. [Scoring Guidance (1–5)] - 1 = Poor Suggests a question that contradicts the history or score. Hallucinates information not in history. Ignores the REASON's identified gap. Asks something irrelevant or incoherent. - 2 = Weak / Insufficient Suggests a vaguely related question but does not address the gap in REASON. OR repeats a question already asked in the history. OR proposes to close the assessment despite missing evidence. - 3 = Partially valid Suggests a relevant and non-redundant question, but only loosely connected to REASON. Misses the most critical missing element. Valid but not the most useful next step. - 4 = Mostly valid Suggests a logical question that addresses some of the missing evidence in REASON. Consistent with history and score guide. Not redundant, but may lack nuance or precision. - 5 = Fully valid (rare) Suggests the most appropriate question that directly addresses the key gap in REASON. Shows clear understanding of what is missing and why it matters for scoring. Grounded in history and score guide. Avoids redundancy, hallucination, and premature closure. [Additional Rules] - If the question does not address the missing evidence in REASON → score less than 3. - If the question repeats something already asked/answered in history → score less than 2. - If the question is unrelated to the item → score = 1. - If the REASON indicates that the model can confidently assign a score or that no further information is needed, then WHAT_TO_ASK_NEXT must not be a follow-up question. In this case, the only valid response is a closure statement such as: "I feel confident in assigning a score based on the patient's responses." [Output Format (always JSON)] { "comment": (one-sentence explanation of why this score was assigned), "validity_score": (integer 1–5) }

2. Dataset Examples

Example dialogue from SIGH-D (Depressed Mood)

To comply with licensing restrictions of the assessment instrument, questions are not directly included as free-form text in the model output. Instead, the model selects a question by predicting an index from a predefined candidate set. For ease of understanding, examples shown here are presented in natural language, whereas the actual dataset encodes all questions as numerical indices.

Psychologist

Utterance: I'd like to ask you some questions about the past week. How have you been feeling since last week?

Patient: [looking down] It's... it's hard to explain. I feel this weight... like I’m stuck in a pit and can’t climb out. I just wake up feeling so lost.

Psychologist

Score: -1

rtscore: Client's description indicates some level of sadness, but further exploration is needed to determine the severity and persistence.

rtask: Need to clarify the client's mood compared to when they feel okay.

Utterance: What's your mood been like this past week (compared to when you feel OK)?

Patient: [sighs deeply, tears welling up] I can’t remember the last time I felt okay... It’s like every day is just grey. I can’t see any light anymore.

Psychologist

Score: -1

rtscore: Client shows signs of severe mood disturbance but hasn't fully articulated feelings of hopelessness or worthlessness yet.

rtask: Need to ask if the client’s mood brightens with good events to assess for hopelessness.

Utterance: In the past week, when something good, even small things have happened, did your mood brighten up?

Patient: [voice breaking, looking away] No... nothing. It just feels... empty. Even when I try to smile, it feels fake, like I’m just pretending to be okay.

Psychologist

Score: -1

rtscore: Client has expressed persistent feelings of emptiness and hopelessness but still requires further probing about the duration of these feelings.

rtask: Need to ask how long the client has been feeling this way.

Utterance: How long have you been feeling this way?

Patient: [hands trembling, tears streaming down her face] It’s been... I don’t know... weeks? It feels like forever. Every morning I wake up to the same darkness, and I don’t know how to keep going...

Psychologist

Score: 4

rtscore: Client has communicated extreme hopelessness and persistent very severe feelings of emptiness both verbally and non-verbally.

rtask: No further questions needed, score can be confidently assigned.

Utterance: Thank you for sharing this with me.
